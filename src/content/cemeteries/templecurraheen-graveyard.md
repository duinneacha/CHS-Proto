<aside class="project-cta" aria-label="Templecurraheen documentation website">
  <h2 class="project-cta-title">Templecurraheen Graveyard website</h2>
  <p class="project-cta-text">
    Search the burial register, view grave photographs, and explore stories from the graveyard —
    a separate documentation project developed by Aidan Dennehy.
  </p>
  <p class="project-cta-actions">
    <a class="btn btn-project-cta" href="https://templecurraheen.ie/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Visit templecurraheen.ie</a>
  </p>
</aside>

        <img src="/assets/Cemeteries/Templecurraheen%20Graveyard%20Panel.jpg" alt="TemplecurraheenPanel">

    <p class="indentedText">
        A new website now documents the heritage of Templecurraheen (Kilcurfin) Graveyard at Carrigtwohill,
        bringing centuries of local history together in one place that anyone can visit 
        from home. At its heart is a searchable burial register, cross-referenced with 
        photographs of the individual graves so that families tracing their ancestors can 
        see the stones for themselves. The painstaking work of gathering the names from 
        every legible stone was carried out by Cora Finn, who recorded each inscription 
        accompanied by her husband Mike, the caretaker of the graveyard. It was many hours 
        of patient work that made the register possible. The website itself was built and 
        developed by Aidan Dennehy, who brought the register, photographs and stories
        together into a single online resource.
    </p>
   
    <p class="indentedText">
        Alongside the register you'll find a gallery of the graveyard through the seasons, and
        a growing Stories section that gathers the local history bound up with the place: the 
        Collins brothers killed at the Tithe War massacre of Gortroe in 1834, the unmarked Famine
        plot at the back of the old graveyard, the Penal-Times story behind the burial of Catholic
        Priest Father John O'Neill of the Castle Lyons Carmelites, the Cotter family of Carhoo 
        House, a rare Irish-language verse from an 18<sup>th</sup> century headstone, and a poem by Rob
        McDonnell on the graveyard itself. The site is the work of the volunteers who have 
        helped re-vitalise Templecurraheen in recent years, and contributions of family memories, 
        photographs and research are very welcome.
    </p>
    <p Class="indentedText">
        The <a href="https://www.echolive.ie/corknews/arid-41849928.html" target="_blank">Evening Echo</a>
        published an article about this very important event from which some of the 
        photographs were provided.
    </p>
