<h1>Local History</h1>
    <h2>Local Stories & Folklore</h2>
    <h3>Ballintubber</h3>
    <p class="indentedText">
        The townland of Ballintubber consists of Ballintubber East and Ballintubber West in the old civil parish of Mogeesha, Barony of
        Barrymore.
    </p>
    <p class="indentedText">
        The Irish name is Baile an Tiobraid – ‘Village of the Well’. There are in fact two wells in the townland.</p>
    <p class="indentedText">
        One of them is located in a field towards the north side of the townland and it is thought that the name of the townland is derived
        from this well of singular character. Known locally as the 40 foot well, it is actually at the bottom of a pit about 30 feet deep and
        descent is by thirty-one stone steps down a sloping gallery lined overhead with great flags. The tout ensemble rather suggests one of
        the sloping passages within the Great Pyramid. To illuminate the depths there are two light shafts. The well may be of considerable 
        antiquity as the place was called Ballintubbrid in the 1500’s.</p>
    <p class="indentedText">
        The other well and the one which was more utilised is located to the south side of the townland. This well supplied the inhabitants 
        of the townland with fresh water up until the 1950’s. The well remains intact and in working order but it’s entrance and surrounds are 
        in much need of some repair.</p>
    <p class="indentedText">
        Before c.1800, the estuary extended up as far as the Well Road and at high tide the sea often covered the well resulting in the local 
        inhabitants having to wait for the tide to go out to fill their wine casks or puncheons with the spring water.</p>
    <p class="indentedText">
        There was a lios on the northern side of the townland which was small and circular, with a rampart five feet high, bisected by a road. 
        The eastern side of the lios, on what was Harte’s farm, had disappeared by c.1800 and the western side of the lios, on Collins’ farm, 
        disappeared c.2000.</p>
    <p class="indentedText">
        Tradition has it that a goat once entered one of the caves at The Rock, just north of the village of Carrigtwohill, emerging in the 
        townland of Ballintubbrid, a few miles southwards. The cave where the goat emerged is called Poll an Ghabhair, meaning The Goat's Hole
        and is located in ‘The Rock’ on the left-hand side of the road at the top of the hill up from Ballintubber Cross.</p>
    <p class="indentedText">
        Hedge schools were small informal illegal schools, particularly in the 18th and 19th century, designed to secretly provide the rudiments
        of primary education to Catholic children. One such school was located at Ballintubber Cross in the 1800’s. There is an account of this
        school given by Barryscourt schoolboy Battie Fitzgerald in an interview with 89 years old John Ahern, also from Barryscourt, which was
        done in 1937/8:</p>
    <p class="IndentQuotes">
        “This school was out door in the Summer and in door in the Winter. Mr. Crowley was teaching in it. He was a local man. The teacher used 
        to go home every night. He used to teach Irish and he liked teaching it as it was chiefly spoken by master and pupil. The writing was
        done on slates with slate pencil. They had a small thatched house and they used sit on the ground in the winter and along the ditch on 
        fine days. The teacher must be there a long time. He had a house at the bottom of Mr. McCarthy's haggard where the ruins are still to be
        seen”.</p>
    <p class="indentedText">
        The field at the South Western corner of ‘The Cross’ is known as the ‘The Barrack Field’. </p>
    <p class="indentedText">
        The Irish National Land League (Conradh na Talún) was an Irish political organisation of the late 19th century which sought to help poor 
        tenant farmers. Its primary aim was to abolish landlordism in Ireland and enable tenant farmers to own the land they worked on. The period
        of the Land League's agitation is known as the Land War. During this time, the English authorities built a small barrack in Ballintubber 
        which was occupied by the Royal Irish Constabulary to ‘protect’ the local landlords and their ‘interests’ from attacks by locals and 
        ‘Whiteboys’. In the late 1870's and early 1880's, Michael Harte from Ballintubber, called "Mike the Yank" was very active in the Land
        League movement in Carrigtwohill and without charge, he spent three months in Clonmel Gaol for his activities. According to my grandfather
        John Joe Harte, Mike the Yank was a “very smart man”. </p>
    <p class="indentedText">
        The Land League had an equivalent organization in the United States, which raised hundreds of thousands of dollars both for famine relief 
        and also for political action. Mike the Yank went to the United States about three times, the purpose being unknown other than visiting his
        brother, sister and first cousins who had emigrated to the Boston region. He returned to Ireland for good around 1900. He died in 1923, 
        unmarried and is buried in the Harte plot in Ballintemple cemetery.</p>
    <p class="indentedText">
        The field on the southern side of the Barrack Field which stretches down the hill towards the well is known as the Well Field. It was said 
        that there was a cemetery in this field c. 1600 but there are no records or evidence of this to confirm that fact.</p>
    <p class="indentedText">
        The field on the southern side of the Barrack Field which stretches down the hill towards the well is known as the Well Field. It was said
        that there was a cemetery in this field c. 1600 but there are no records or evidence of this to confirm that fact.</p>
    <p class="indentedText">
        At the southern edge of Ballintubber lies the ‘The Brown Island’. At high tide it becomes surrounded by the sea. There are two shell middens
        located along the shoreline in this area. One is at the north eastern side of Brown Island and the other is a little further west along the 
        shoreline (between Brick Island and Brown Island) called the Ballintubbrid West midden. These are but two of about 25 such sites dotted around
        Cork Harbour.</p>
    <p class="indentedText">
        From research carried out by Milner & Woodman in 2007, the Ballintubbrid midden is said to consist of oyster shells that date from 200 BC – 
        1030 AD and the Brown Island midden, also of oyster shells, dates from 1470 – 1690 AD.</p>
    <p class="indentedText">
        During Penal Times, under the cover of darkness, the island was used to smuggle whiskey and wool to mainland Europe in return for tea, wine 
        and tobacco. Smuggling was a huge trade at that time, even students of the priesthood were smuggled to Rome and France due to the closure
        of Irish seminaries under the Penal Laws.</p>
    <p class="indentedText">
        Brown Island Quay (Heard’s Quay) was built in the 1880’s for boats bringing timber, coal, wool and grain across the estuary, to and from 
        Ballinacurra.</p>
    <p class="indentedText">
        Balynetuberidroyhtrighe was named in the list of places burned by Hugh O’Neill, Earl of Tyrone on his march south in 1599, during the Nine
        Years War. In January 1600, The Four Masters tell us that, Hugh O’Neill, ‘proceeded to the south of Ireland, to confirm his friendship with
        his allies in the war, and to wreak his vengeance upon his enemies’. Most of the Irish lords submitted to O’Neill, with the exception of 
        David Barry who was loyal to the Queen and whose territory O’Neill made an example of and, ‘traversed, plundered, and burned it, from one 
        extremity to the other, both plain and wood, both level and rugged, so that no one hoped or expected that it could be inhabited for a long 
        time afterwards’.</p>
    <p class="indentedText">
        The townland was named Ballintubbrid in the reign of Elizabeth I and remained so called until at least the 1911 Census of Ireland.</p>
    <p class="indentedText">
        Following the 1656-1658 Down Survey of Ireland, we can see that David Fitz-David Barry earl of Barrymore (Protestant) was the owner of the 
        townland of Ballintrobid in both the years 1641 and 1670. Francis Gould of Cork (Catholic) was the owner of West Ballytobrid in 1641 and 
        Sir John Broderick (Protestant) was the owner in 1670. This reflected the fact that following Cromwell’s victory in Ireland, there was a 
        massive transfer of landownership from Irish Catholics to English Protestants.</p>
    <p class="indentedText">
        It was listed as Ballytuberid in the 1659 Census of Ireland, having 35 Irish inhabitants in East Ballytuberid and 7 Irish inhabitants in 
        West Ballytuberid. There were no English people living there at that time.</p>

    <p class="indentedText">
        The above notice refers to 386 acres of land at Ballintubbrid East.</p>
    <p class="indentedText">
        Mr. William Weekes was known to be the landlord of Ballintubbrid East in 1814. Dr. Edward Heard was landlord of the townland, as recorded
        in the Tithe Applotment Books dated 14th April 1834. He was succeeded by his son Richard. The Heard family, who had come from Kinsale,
        remained as landlords up until the 1930’s.
</p>
    <p class="indentedText">
        Lord Midleton was the landlord of the townland of Ballintubbrid West, as recorded also in the Tithe Applotment Books dated 1834.</p>
    <p class="indentedText">
        The Brodricks, who held the title Viscount Midleton, were an English family that had ‘settled’ in Ireland in the mid-17th century as part of Cromwell’s
        infamous Irish campaign. They ‘obtained’ large swathes of land in Imokilly. The family's original seat was Ballyannan Castle near Midleton, which 
        they occupied until c1728 upon the death of Alan Brodrick 1st Viscount Midleton, but they continued to own the castle, becoming a ruin by 1837. 
        After 1728, they were ‘absentee landlords’ and resided in England.</p>
    <p class="indentedText">
        In the 1901 Census, Ballintubbrid East had 97 residents and Ballintubbrid West had 56 residents.</p>
    <p class="indentedText">
        In the 1911 Census, Ballintubbrid East had 81 residents and Ballintubbrid West had 50 residents.</p>
    <p class="indentedText">
        The farm down by the sea shore consisting of the Brown Island was owned by John Harte (no relation) in the early 1800’s and subsequently
        by his son Patrick and grandson John. It was bought by Sean Leahy of Ballyannon, who was a brother of Donie Collins’ grandfather’s second
        wife, in the 1930’s and the ‘strand’ was referred to as ‘Sean’s’ from then onwards. Sean tilled the land and had a harvest threshing every
        year. The steam engine and thresher would come to the threshing site via Mulcahy’s house towards the sea and then steamed left behind the
        sea bank for half a mile or so before arriving beneath the hill where Donie Collins’ house is now. As Sean got on in years, he stayed the
        night at the Collins home in North Ballintubber and cycled up and down every day to the farm. He was a droll character with a great sense 
        of humour.</p>
    <p class="indentedText">
        There was a sea wall erected along the strand in the early 1800’s. My father Dan and my uncle Johnnie, when working as young farm hands at
        Buckley’s of Clyduff, along with other men working there in the early forties, recall driving a large flock of sheep down to be washed
        in the sea at the western side of the sea wall. The strong men would change and don old clothes and boots. They would drag the sheep 
        into the sea at high tide and with strong hands rub the wool until it was clean. The sheep would then be shorn in the following few days.</p>
    <p class="indentedText">
        Many of the local farmers would cut ‘oar weed’ from the strand and bring it back to their fields to be used as manure. Some had their own 
        stretch of seashore but most got it from Sean Leahy’s strand and paid him by the load for it.</p>
    <p class="indentedText">
        Many years have passed since Tyrone burned every farmhouse in Ballintubber. Many generations have made it their home in the intervening 
        centuries, many families were born, raised and died there and many more who left continued and still continue to this day to call it ‘Home’.
</p>
