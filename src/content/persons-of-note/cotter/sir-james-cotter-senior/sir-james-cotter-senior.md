<h1>Cotter of Anngrove & Rockforest</h1>
    <h2>Cotter of Cotter&#39;s Lodge Anngrove (Ballinsperrig)</h2>
    <h3>Colonel Sir James Cotter </h3>
    <figure class="FigureRight">
        <img src="/assets/Persons%20of%20Note/Cotter/James%20Cotter/BethamsCrest.jpg" alt="ImageBethamsChrest">
    <figcaption>Betham's Will Extracts</figcaption>
    </figure>
    <p class="indentedText">
        Soon after the death of Edmond Cotter of Ballinsperrig, Carrigtwohill, the JCHAS of 1965 tells us, the “Glorious
        Revolution” came with the fall of Catholic society. His Son, Colonel Sir James was a gentleman of considerable means, 
        who had received many marks of favours from brother Kings, Charles II and James II, and was remarkable for his warm
        attachment to the Stuart family. Sir James began his military career as an officer in the King’s life guards, rising 
        through the ranks, attaining the rank of Colonel.
    </p>
    <p class="indentedText">
        The Restoration of the Stuart monarchy in the kingdoms of England, Scotland and Ireland took place in 1660
        when King Charles II returned from exile in continental Europe. Charles II formally became Catholic on his 
        deathbed in 1685. James II became catholic in the 1660s along with his first wife Anne Hyde.
    </p>
  
    <p class="indentedText">
        There are many documented accounts of Sir James Cotters political and military life, most notably within 
        the Journals of Cork Historical and Archaeological Society (JCHAS), so no need to duplicate here.
        </p>
   
    <p class="indentedText">
        Burke’s Peerage tells us Colonel Sir James married firstly Mary, relict of Abraham Anselme, esq., of the City of 
        London, and daughter of Sir William Stapleton, Bart., Governor General of the Leeward Islands. Then, 30<sup>th</sup> July 1688, Eleanora Plunkett, eldest daughter
        of Mathew, 7<sup>th</sup> Earl of Louth (by Jane, daughter of Sir Luke FitzGerald, of Terroghan). From this union came James (hanged), Laurence, Mary, Alice and
        Monica. He is the patrilinear ancestor of the Cotter baronets of Rockforest.
    </p>
       <figure class="FigureLeft">
        <img src="/assets/Persons%20of%20Note/Cotter/Sir%20James%20Cotter%20Senior/Gould%20-%20Vicar%20General%20Marriage%20Licences.jpg" alt="ImageMarriageLicence">
    <figcaption>Gould - Vicar General Marriage Licences</figcaption>
    </figure>
    <p class="indentedText">
        Our own research in fact shows Sir James first marriage took place, 3<sup>rd</sup> June 1679, at the famous Protestant
        Church of St. Martin-in-the-Fields, Trafalgar Square, London. He was aged about 40, having him born circa 1639.
        It is very likely Garrett was his brother, but nothing is currently known of Mathew. Yet, James appears to have ‘alleged’
        the marriage according to alongside Gould Extracts. No record has yet been found for his second marriage to Eleanor. She 
        of course was closely related to the martyred Saint Oliver Plunket of Drogheda.
    </p>
    <p class="indentedText">
        Rev. Charles Bernard Gibson documents Ballinsperrig land within his History of County and City of Cork, 1862, Vol II, reprinted
        by Journal of the Royal Society of Antiquaries of Ireland, Volume 89, Part 2, which tells us that David, 1<sup>st</sup> Earl of Barrymore, 
        mortgaged the castle, farm and lands of Ballinsperrig and Lacken north of Killeagh, to Sir James Cotter for the sum of £300. This
        statement is in error, as has already been stated above, he had died 1842. Anngrove, also known as Cotter’s Lodge, was the property
        he leased from, Richard, 2<sup>nd </sup>Earl of Barrymore. Other properties on Great Island Sir James had purchased in 1665 from the Earl 
        became the subject of a series of lawsuits between Sir James Cotter and Lord Barrymore.
    </p>
    <h3>Death of Sir James</h3>
    <figure class="FigureLeft">
        <img src="/assets/Persons%20of%20Note/Cotter/Sir%20James%20Cotter%20Senior/Betham%27s%20Sir%20Cotter%20Abstract.jpg" alt="ImageBetham">
    <figcaption>Betham's Will Extracts</figcaption>
    </figure>
    <p class="indentedText">
        There has long been much controversy as to whether or not James held an hereditary title from his father. For his
        grandson of Rockforest, Sir James Laurence, Burke’s Peerage includes the family heraldry crest. It is the same crest 
        Betham gives to illustrate the abstract for his father’s proved Will of 1705. It had been stated by Burke’s and 
        subsequently repeated by many publications that Sir James died in 1705, a statement it is believed made in error. 
        Betham’s Wills abstracts clearly suggests, Sir James of ‘Cotter’s Lodge’ died, 15 <sup>th</sup>February 1703 with
        his Will being proved, 17 <sup>th</sup> August 1705. Also mentioned were his niece, Anne, daughter of his brother, 
        John; his niece Eleanor Barry; and his late wife the daughter of Mathew Lord Baron of Louth; his sons James and 
        Laurence; and daughter Mary. Lastly was his brother-in-law of Terrysland, Michael Durdin, so presumably his sister 
        had died by this time.
    </p>
    <p class="indentedText">
        At the City Archives, a deed of indenture (U681/60/50) for the lands at Terrysland dated, 1<sup>st</sup> September 1704,
        between Sir James Cotter and Michael Durdin, and has been fully transcribed. How could the Colonel sign a document when 
        already in his grave? It could only have been his hanged son, Sir James!</p>
      <p class="indentedText">
        There is a passage of interest within Richard Caulfield’s "Annals of the Cathedral of St. Coleman, Cloyne", 
        published in Cork, 1882, further reinforcing this theory.</p>
    <p class="IndentQuotes">
        “Dr. Crow was a prelate of great learning and munificence, as will hereafter appear. In 1703 the Dean and 
        Chapter confirm a grant of Sir James Cotter’s burying place. This Sir James represented the City of Cork in
        Parliament, and was Collector of Customs for the port. He married, in July, 1689, Eleanor Plunkett, daughter
        of Matthew, the seventh Lord Louth….”</p>
    <p class="indentedText">
        In his will, Sir James Cotter stipulated he shall be buried in Kilcurfin (Templecurraheen) with his three 
        children that had already buried in Carrigtwohill will be reinterred with him stating:
    </p>
    <p class="IndentQuotes">
        “not doubting that my son and heir, when permitted to finish the burying place I began in the Church of 
        Carrigtwohill, and of which I was hindered, will remove mine and my said childrens bodies thither, which
        I desire and appoint may be done with all convenient speed after finishing the said buying place.” </p>
  
    
    <p class="indentedText">
        An entry in the Directory of Irish Biography by Vincent Morley states:</p>
    <p class="IndentQuotes">
        The foreword to ‘Párliament na mBan’ informs us that the bishop of Cork, John Baptist Sleyne (1639?–1712), was 
        sheltered for more than three years at Ballinsperry and that the catholic clergy frequently assembled there....
        Cotter died 18 June 1705 and was lamented in a poem by Liam Mac Cairteáin (qv).</p>
