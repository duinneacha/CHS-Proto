    <h3>Site Location & Tendering Process</h3>
    <figure class="FigureLeft">
        <img src="/assets/Schools/Boys%20School/Terry%20Lease%201893.jpg" alt="ImageMap">
    <figcaption>Lease Agreement Map, <a href="http://www.corkarchives.ie/" target="_blank">Cork City Archives</a>, U229/71/13/3</figcaption>
    </figure>
    <p class="indentedText">
        In 1957 the new Boys' National School (<a href="https://carrigtwohill.com/" target="_blank">Scoil Mhuire Naofa</a>)
        was built to replace the one <a href="/schools/first-national-school/first-school/">Father Richard Seymour</a>
        built a century earlier. For eighty years his school catered for the need of all the children of the 
        parish until 1937, when the girls were transferred to their new school erected by Canon Bowler, P. P.</p>
    <p class="indentedText">
        Included within a lease agreement documents held at, Cork City Archives, between Michael Hennessy and 
        spinsters Mary E Terry and Mary X Terry, 1896, describes the same area in great detail, is Solicitor's 
        plan of the relevant location lying behind the the school's original frontage; bearing no resemblance to any
        online OSI mapping system, which abuts the land once owned by Johanna Seymour, granted her upon the death
        of her brother, Father Richard Seymour. The lease refers to the area highlighted as, 71, 71A, 71B, and 74A.
        71B and 74A now being the property of Carrigtwohill GAA. However, the area along the Main Street marked by
        'X' appears to be a separate property not mentioned in the Lease.</p>
    <p class="indentedText">
        The OSI map used for the Griffith’s Valuation of 1852, appears to show a Parochial School in this location.
        Then the map of the same area of 1889 seems to show a few cottages. More research is required to properly 
        identify ownership of this piece of land.</p>
       
    <p class="indentedText">
        Much difficulty was experienced by the local community to secure a suitable location for the parish’s new school
        for the boys. Eventually, after nearly ten years struggle, Father Henry Roche, P. P., was able to procure the plot
        of land at the western end of the village, upon which, in May of 1956 construction works began.
    </p>
    <figure class="FigureRight">
        <img src="/assets/Schools/Boys%20School/New%20School%20Tenders%20-%20CE%2021%20Dec%201955.jpg" alt="">
        <figcaption>Cork Examiner, 21<sup>st</sup> December 1955</figcaption>
    </figure>
    <p class="indentedText">
        In early December 1955, sealed tenders were invited for the erection of a new National Boys’ school at Carrigtwohill 
        by the ‘Office of Public Works’, Dublin, for the price of £1, refundable upon the return of the documents. 
        They could also be viewed at Father Henry J. Roche's Parochial house, who was the brother of Bishop James of
        Cloyne & Ross, who had a passionate interest in this proposed school project, and brought every pressure to 
        bear on the authorities for the urgent need for this replacement school. Sadly, the Bishop did not see 
        the completion of these works, as he had died suddenly at Queenstown, 31<sup>st</sup> November, the following
        year. The date recorded is obviously incorrect, as November only has 30 days.
    </p>
    <p class="indentedText">
        The Department of Education contributed 5/6<sup>th</sup> of the build cost, with the remainder being realised through voluntary contribution
        from the local, and neighbouring communities; the construction of which was an entirely local affair whose contract
        was awarded to Mr. Barnabas Monahan of Barryscourt, who employed local labour, and was supplied with materials procured within the immediate locality.</p>
     
    <p class="indentedText">
        Henry Roche died 21 Nov 1957</p>
