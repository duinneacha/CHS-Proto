<h1>Down's Survey of Ireland</h1>
    <h2>A general outline</h2>
    <h3>The Irish rebellion</h3>
    <p class="indentedText">
        The outbreak of the Irish rebellion, October 1641, was as an attempted coup d'état by the Irish Catholic gentry,
        in an effort to gain control of Irish administration, forcing the British government to grant concessions to the 
        Catholic majority living under British rule. However, the coup failed the following year resulting in ethnic conflict 
        between native Irish Catholics British Protestant settlers; a conflict known as the Irish Confederate Wars. The 
        Confederation became a de facto government of most of Ireland, loosely aligned with the Royalist viewpoint. The subsequent 
        war continued in Ireland until the 1650s, when Oliver Cromwell decisively defeated the Irish Catholics and Royalists; thus
        the re-conquering of Ireland.</p>
    <p class="indentedText">
        As a consequence of the failed rebellion, the Down Survey of Ireland, was undertaken between 1656 and 1658; the first 
        detailed survey of its type anywhere in the world; the objective of which was to fully document all forfeited Irish Catholic
        property for redistribution to British Merchant and Loyal soldiers.  </p>
    <p class="indentedText">
        Trinity College, Dublin, have brought together for the first time in over 300 years all the surviving maps, digitising them,
        to have them available online via their <a href="http://downsurvey.tcd.ie/" target="_blank">website</a>, from which the
        following are extracts.</p>

    <h3>Downs Survey Map of Cork</h3>

    <p class="indentedText">
        It is interesting to note spelling as "Carituoghill", with all parish townlands falling within the coloured borders which
        consisting of a little over 3694 acres.
    </p>
    <p class="indentedText">
        The last entry of the Parish Occupants table following shows “P” which denotes the Protestant Parish owned by 
        Lord Barrimore, naming it as Fforestowne, consisting of 188 acres. Viscount David Barry (1604–1642) had died 
        by this time. His son, James (1630–1694), was the Earl of Barrymore.
    </p>
    <p class="indentedText">
        It is difficult to see from this map, but to the top left (northern corner) of Ballinsperry (71) owned by
        are two plots; Labane of 7 acres, owned by O’Connell (77); and immediately to its right is the Glebe land
        of 5 acres.
    </p>
    <p class="indentedText">
        By the time of the Griffith’s Valuation of 1852 Forrest-Town was recorded as consisting of 124 acres; Labaun
        had 13; and Kilcurfin Glebe had 10.
    </p>
