When coming from Midleton at the roundabout before joining the N25, take a left on the road to Ballyannan and Ballyvodock. The ruin of Ballyannan House and Castle stands on the right — a silent presence in the landscape that has been ruinous for over two and a half centuries.

## The Brodricks and their castle

The castle was built in 1650, and the St John Brodrick family were its first occupants. By 1786, within little more than a century of its construction, it was already described as being in ruins. The speed of its decline speaks to the turbulence of the period — the upheavals of the Williamite wars and the subsequent dispossession of many landed families left much of this part of Cork re-organised under new ownership within a generation.

By 1814 the site was occupied by Roger Williams. At the time of Griffith's Valuation in the mid-nineteenth century, it was recorded as valued at £9.10, held by Daniel Twomey under Lord Midleton.

## What remains — and what doesn't

To the left of the castle ruin is a small tidal inlet that was once a graveyard. Within living memory, bones were found there during earthworks — a quiet reminder that the dead were placed in this corner of the parish for centuries before the records caught up with them.

There are also reports that a silver hoard was found when a nearby field was ploughed — silver spoons, evidence perhaps of a household in flight or a hiding concealed before calamity.

The most intriguing local tradition is the account of a cave running from the side of the castle all the way to Ahanesk. No such passage has been formally surveyed, but the story persists.

## Getting there

Take the R630 from Midleton towards Carrigtwohill. At the first roundabout, take the left for Ballyannan and Ballyvodock. The ruin is visible from the road on the right-hand side.
