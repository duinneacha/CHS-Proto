Barryscourt Castle stands at the southern edge of Carrigtwohill village, a compact and formidable tower-house that has survived eight centuries of Irish history in better condition than almost any comparable structure in Munster. Today it is maintained by the Office of Public Works and is open to visitors; for most of its life, it was the seat of one of the most powerful Norman-Irish dynasties in East Cork.

## The Barrys of East Cork

The castle takes its name from the Barry family — specifically the line known as the Barry Mór — who arrived in Ireland with the Norman invasion of the twelfth century and established themselves across a wide swathe of County Cork. At the height of their influence, they controlled territory stretching from the Lee to the Blackwater. Carrigtwohill was at the heart of their holdings.

The tower-house that stands today dates largely from the fifteenth century, though there were earlier structures on the site. It follows the standard Munster tower-house plan: a tall, rectangular keep with corner towers, a defended bawn or courtyard, and the vertical organisation of domestic and defensive space that characterised elite Irish architecture of the period.

## Tower-house, bawn, and castle life

The keep rises to five storeys. The ground floor was used for storage; the upper floors contained the great hall, the lord's private chambers, and sleeping quarters for the household. Corner turrets at the upper levels provided both lookout positions and some private space — a luxury rare in medieval Irish buildings.

The bawn to the south enclosed outbuildings, a well, and the domestic machinery of a working castle household. The wall-walk and flanking towers of the bawn are largely intact, making Barryscourt one of the most complete surviving examples of a defended Irish domestic complex of this period.

## Decline and restoration

The castle passed out of the Barrys' hands in the seventeenth century, caught up in the general confiscation of Gaelic and Old English lands following the Cromwellian and Williamite campaigns. It was used as a farmyard for much of the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries, which paradoxically helped preserve its fabric — the walls were too useful as shelter and storage to be quarried for building stone.

A major conservation and restoration programme was undertaken by the OPW in the 1990s, which stabilised the structure and furnished some of the interior spaces according to the archaeological and historical record.

## Visiting

Barryscourt Castle is signposted from the N25 at Carrigtwohill and is a short walk from the village centre. It is one of the must-see historical sites of East Cork, and the OPW has produced excellent interpretive material on-site.
